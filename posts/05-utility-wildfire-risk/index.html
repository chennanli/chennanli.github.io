Wildfire Risk for the Grid: A Decision Workflow, Not a Crystal Ball

How weather, terrain, fire history, and — above all — honest model validation come together to support grid-hardening and power-shutoff decisions. A working demo on public data, and what it would take to make it real.

By Chennan Li, PhD, PE · July 2026 · Personal project, public data only

After the January 2025 Eaton Fire, every electric utility operating in fire country has been forced to answer one uncomfortable question with real money on the line: of all the miles of power line we own, which spans are the most likely to start the next catastrophic fire, and what should we do about them first? It sounds like a machine-learning problem, and there is machine learning inside the answer, but the longer I spent building a working demo of it, the more clearly I saw that this is really a decision problem that happens to use a model as one of its ingredients — and that the hardest and most valuable part had almost nothing to do with the model itself.

This post walks through a small end-to-end project I built, called Ember, using only public data on a personal laptop. I’m writing it less as a tutorial on how to train a classifier and more as a case study in how a utility actually turns weather, terrain, and fire history into billion-dollar hardening plans and same-day power shut-offs — and in why the thing that separates a credible model from a dangerous one is not its cleverness but the discipline of how it’s checked.

The whole workflow on one page. Ember turns public data into a single risk number for every span on every day, ranks those numbers by how much risk each dollar buys back, and hands a portfolio owner two kinds of decision: what to harden over the next few years, and where to cut power tonight. The dashed bar is what SEPA’s 2026 utility-tech framework calls the real gap — integration and validation, not a fancier model — and it’s the part this project is really about.

1The question utilities are actually trying to answer

Picture the person who ultimately owns this problem inside a utility, and notice that it is almost never a data scientist. It is a portfolio owner — an engineering or operations leader who has been handed a hardening budget measured in billions of dollars and a service territory with tens of thousands of miles of overhead line running through canyons, brush, and neighborhoods, and who has to decide, defensibly and in front of a regulator, which of those miles get rebuilt this year and which have to wait. On the worst wind days that same leader also has to decide whether to trigger a Public Safety Power Shut-off, which is the deliberate act of cutting power to a community so that a spark from the utility’s own equipment can’t start a fire — a decision that keeps people safe but also strands medical equipment, traffic lights, and refrigerators full of food, so it can’t be made casually or too often.

What makes this genuinely hard is the shape of the thing being predicted. Equipment-caused ignitions are extraordinarily rare on any single span on any single day, yet when one does occur under the wrong wind it can grow into a fire that kills people, destroys towns, and threatens the survival of the company itself, so the leader is being asked to rank an enormous number of very ordinary-looking assets by a very rare, very high-consequence event. That combination — huge population, tiny base rate, catastrophic tail — is exactly the setting where intuition fails and where a model can genuinely help, provided the model is honest about how little any single prediction can really promise.

2One simple equation behind a very expensive decision

Underneath all the sophistication, the industry organizes this problem around one clean idea that a non-technical executive can hold in their head, which is that the risk a given span poses on a given day is the product of two separate questions multiplied together. The first is the probability of ignition: given today’s wind and dryness, this span’s vegetation and terrain, and the age and type of the equipment, how likely is it to start a fire at all? The second is the consequence: if it starts one, how far and how fast would that fire run given the fuel and the weather, and how many people and structures sit in its path? Multiply the two and you get a single risk number that is finally comparable across every span and every day, which is the whole point — you cannot compare a rickety line over empty desert against a newer line above a crowded foothill town until you’ve expressed both of them in the same units.

In plain termsA line that almost never sparks but sits above a town, and a line that sparks often but sits over bare rock, can end up with the same risk score. The equation exists precisely so that a leader isn’t forced to compare apples to oranges by gut feel, and so that the reasoning behind a spending decision can be written down and defended.

But knowing the risk of every span still doesn’t tell you where to spend, because the most dangerous span isn’t automatically the smartest place to put the next dollar, and this is where the second layer of the equation matters. Utilities rank their options by Risk Spend Efficiency — the amount of risk a project removes divided by what it costs — because a regulator reviewing a multi-billion-dollar plan will always ask why a given dollar went here instead of somewhere it would have bought back more safety. The counter-intuitive result this surfaces, over and over, is that the intervention which removes the most risk in absolute terms is often the worst value for money, which is exactly the kind of truth that a gut-feel process buries and that an explicit efficiency ranking drags into the open.

Real Ember data. Mitigations ranked by risk removed per dollar. The cheap tuning options sit at the top; undergrounding the line removes the largest absolute chunk of risk yet lands at the bottom on efficiency — the tension every wildfire mitigation plan has to resolve out loud in front of a regulator.

3What I built: Ember, a small end-to-end demo

Ember is my attempt to build that entire chain — from raw public data all the way to a ranked list of where to act — small enough to run on a laptop but complete enough that every link in the workflow is real rather than hand-waved. It pulls in publicly available fire history, terrain, weather, and vegetation, learns an ignition model from it, attaches a consequence estimate, multiplies the two into a risk score, and then ranks the spans by risk-per-dollar, which is the same skeleton a commercial system uses even though a real one is fed far richer and largely private data.

The one modeling choice that shapes everything downstream is the unit of analysis, and it’s worth stating plainly because it’s where a lot of the honesty of the project lives. Rather than asking “is this line risky?” in the abstract, Ember chops the modeled grid into short segments and then looks at each segment on each day of the fire season, so the fundamental row in the dataset is a single “segment-day” — this span, on this date, under that day’s weather. In this build that comes out to 280 modeled segments observed across 2,354 fire-season dates, which is 659,120 segment-days in total, and framing the data this way is what lets the model learn that the same physical span can be perfectly safe on a calm damp morning and genuinely dangerous the following dry, windy afternoon.

A deliberate boundary runs through this whole project: the labels — which segment-days actually saw a fire — come from public CAL FIRE fire perimeters, while everything a real utility knows about its own equipment (inspection findings, outage history, asset age, vegetation clearance) is private and absent here. Ember is honest demonstration built on open data, not a stand-in for a utility’s internal model, and the rest of this post keeps that line visible.

4What the data can and cannot tell us

The first thing the data does is humble you, because when you count how many of those 659,120 segment-days actually coincided with a fire, the answer is just 283 — four hundredths of one percent — and that single fact quietly dictates almost every technical decision that follows. A model that shrugs and predicts “no fire” for every span on every day would be right 99.96% of the time and would also be completely worthless, which is why “accuracy” is a trap in this domain and why the real job is to find a vanishingly rare needle without burying the operators under a haystack of false alarms they’ll learn to ignore.

Real Ember data. Of 659,120 segment-days only 283 were fires, so the chart needs a log scale just to make the red sliver visible at all — on a normal axis it would vanish. This is the imbalance that makes plain “accuracy” meaningless and forces you toward metrics that reward catching the rare event.

What the data can tell you, once you stop chasing accuracy, is pattern: it can learn that steep slopes, dry fuel, and days of high wind and low humidity cluster together in the moments before ignitions, and it can reproduce the broad geography of where fires have historically started, which is genuinely useful for steering attention. What it fundamentally cannot see is the specific cracked insulator, the corroded splice, or the particular dead tree that will fall onto a conductor next Tuesday, because that knowledge doesn’t live in public weather and terrain rasters at all — it lives in a utility’s private inspection and asset records, and no amount of clever modeling on open data can conjure it into existence. Keeping that distinction sharp is what stops a demo like this from quietly overpromising.

5The score that looked too good to be true

The most important hour of this entire project was the one where I nearly fooled myself, and I’m telling it in full because it is the part that actually matters for anyone deciding whether to trust a model like this. My first working version scored an AUC of about 0.99 — a near-perfect ranking — and for a little while I believed it, right up until I stopped to ask why it was doing so well and realized the model was effectively memorizing which fires had happened where, because segments belonging to the same historical fire were landing in both the data it trained on and the data I was testing it against, so it was being quietly graded on questions it had already seen the answers to.

What “AUC” means, onceThink of AUC as a ranking score between a coin flip at 0.5 and a perfect ranking at 1.0: if you pick one real fire-day and one calm day at random, it’s the chance the model scores the fire-day as riskier. A 0.99 sounds spectacular precisely because, in a problem this hard, it’s almost always a sign that something has leaked.

The fix was to change how the model is graded rather than the model itself: I re-ran the validation so that entire fires are held out at once — the model is trained on some fires and then tested only on completely different fires it has never encountered, which is the situation it will always face in the real world — and the score promptly fell from 0.99 to about 0.65. That drop felt like bad news for about a minute and then felt like the most valuable result in the project, because 0.65 is the honest number and 0.99 was a comfortable illusion, and a modeler who walks into an executive’s office reporting the 0.99 is either fooling themselves or the people paying them, neither of which is survivable when the downstream decision is whether to leave a town’s power on during a windstorm.

Real Ember data. The identical model scored two ways. The red 0.99 came from letting the same fire appear in training and testing — it leaked. The green 0.65, with its honest uncertainty bar, is what the model can really do when it’s forced to generalize to fires it has never seen. The gap between those two bars is the whole lesson of the project.

6Why trust is a product requirement, not a nice-to-have

Once you accept that the point of this work is to support real spending and real shut-off decisions, trust stops being a soft virtue and becomes a hard requirement you have to engineer for, and it shows up in two concrete properties that I checked directly. The first is calibration, which asks a very literal question: when the model says a span has a three-percent chance of igniting, do roughly three percent of such spans actually go on to burn? This matters because the whole workflow multiplies these probabilities by dollars and by populations, and if the numbers are systematically inflated or deflated then every downstream comparison is quietly corrupted no matter how good the ranking looks.

Calibration. When Ember says “3%,” do about 3% really burn? Points sitting on the dashed line are honest probabilities — and honest probabilities are what let you multiply risk by money and trust the result enough to spend on it.
Capture. If you inspect the riskiest spans first, how quickly do you catch the real fires? At the top 10% of risk Ember catches about 15.5% of them — better than the 10% you’d get by luck, but only modestly, which is the honest state of a governance demo rather than a finished prioritizer.

The second property is whether the model catches real fires when you actually use it the way an operator would, which is to work down a ranked list from riskiest to safest, and here the honest result is deliberately unglamorous: inspecting the top ten percent of spans by Ember’s risk score turns up about 15.5% of the real fires, which beats the ten percent you’d expect from random selection but only by a modest margin. I’m reporting that number rather than hiding it because it tells the truth about what this is — a credible governance demonstration on public data, not yet a production-grade prioritizer — and because a leader deciding whether to fund the next stage deserves to know the real starting point, not a polished one. It also helps that the features the model leans on most, when you probe them, are the ones fire science says should matter — slope, dryness, fire weather — which is a reassuring sign that the model learned physics rather than noise, and that reassurance is itself a form of trust you can put in front of a skeptical engineer.

7What a real utility would add before betting on this

If the goal were to move Ember from an honest demo to something a utility would actually stake decisions on, the gap is not really about a fancier algorithm — it’s about data and validation that only the utility itself possesses. The next layers, in roughly the order they’d move the needle, would be the utility’s proprietary asset history and inspection records, its outage and past-ignition history, its vegetation-clearance data, higher-resolution fuel and weather feeds than the public rasters allow, its own history of when and where it has called shut-offs, and — underpinning all of it — blind validation on later fire seasons that the model has genuinely never touched, so that the honest 0.65 either climbs or doesn’t under conditions nobody can fake.

It’s also worth being clear about where a project like this sits relative to the commercial world, because Southern California Edison and its peers already buy sophisticated fire-spread modeling from established vendors, so an open, from-scratch build like Ember is not a competitor to those systems and isn’t trying to be. Its value is as proof that I understand the engine well enough to reason about it, to interrogate a vendor’s claims, and to explain to operators and executives what the numbers do and don’t mean — which brings up the single most important framing of the whole exercise, that this is decision support, not autopilot. A human portfolio owner still makes every call; the model’s job is only to make those calls better-informed, more consistent, and defensible in front of the regulator, and a system that quietly tried to take the human out of that loop would be failing at the actual assignment rather than excelling at it.

8What I took away from building it

The lesson I keep coming back to is that wildfire risk modeling is far less about constructing a mysterious artificial intelligence that foresees fires and far more about building a decision workflow that a wide cast of people — field engineers, control-room operators, regulators, and executives who control the budget — can actually trust and act on. The model is one replaceable component in that workflow, and the parts that genuinely determine whether it’s usable turned out to be the unglamorous ones: validating it in a way that can’t leak, calibrating its probabilities so they can be multiplied by money, reporting a modest capture rate without dressing it up, and being able to explain every one of those choices to someone who will never read a line of the code.

If there’s a single sentence I’d want a hiring manager or a colleague to take from this, it’s that the hardest skill on display here wasn’t training the classifier, which is nearly a commodity now, but knowing which number to believe when one version of the model offered me a flattering 0.99 and the honest version offered a humbler 0.65 — and choosing, without hesitation, to build the rest of the decision on the number that was true.

+Technical appendix — for the modelers in the room

Everything above was written so a non-technical leader could follow it end to end; this appendix is the part I’d hand to a fellow modeler who wants to know exactly what sits under the hood, and it’s deliberately kept separate so it doesn’t clutter the argument.

The ignition model is a gradient-boosted decision-tree classifier (scikit-learn’s HistGradientBoostingClassifier) trained on the segment-day table, chosen because it handles the mixed, non-linear, partly-missing tabular features of terrain, weather, and vegetation gracefully and cheaply. The severe class imbalance — 283 positives in 659,120 rows — is handled through class weighting rather than naive resampling, and the probabilities are calibrated after training so that the model’s stated risk actually tracks observed frequency, which is what makes the calibration curve above meaningful rather than decorative.

The validation story hinges entirely on grouped cross-validation: rather than splitting segment-days at random, whole fires are treated as groups and held out together (a leave-one-fire-out style split), which is the difference between the leaky 0.99 and the honest grouped-CV AUC of roughly 0.65 ± 0.12. Randomly splitting segment-days lets fragments of the same fire sit on both sides of the train/test line and is the single most common way a model like this flatters itself; grouping by fire is the fix, and reporting the uncertainty band alongside the point estimate keeps the honesty visible.

Real Ember data. Permutation importance — how far the model’s skill drops when each input is scrambled. Slope leads (fire runs uphill), followed by dryness and fire-weather, matching what fire science predicts, which is the evidence that the model learned structure rather than memorizing noise.

On the consequence side, the demo uses a transparent spread-and-exposure proxy driven by fuel, slope, and wind, standing in for the calibrated fire-spread simulators that commercial vendors run; the interface is deliberately a drop-in so that a heavier physics-based or cellular-automata spread model can replace the proxy without touching the rest of the pipeline. The prioritization layer implements Risk Spend Efficiency directly and carries a benefit-cost companion, which is the language regulators use when they scrutinize a Wildfire Mitigation Plan, and the whole thing is wired so that swapping any single stage — a better ignition model, richer consequence physics, real proprietary features — leaves the surrounding decision workflow intact. That modularity is the point: the value was never any one model, it was the honest, inspectable pipeline around it.
